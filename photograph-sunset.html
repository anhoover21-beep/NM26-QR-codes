📸 How to Photograph a Desert Sunset

(A Guide to Catching Sky Fire Without Clanking It)

New Mexico sunsets are easy to watch… but tricky to capture. Here’s how to make your photos look like what your eyes actually saw — instead of “why is it all white?” or “why does the sun look like a nuclear flashbang?”


1. Face AWAY From the Sun

Beginners aim the camera at the sun. Don’t. The colors happen in the opposite direction — purples, pinks, gold reflections on the clouds.

Point your camera:

east
northeast
or wherever the clouds are glowing

That’s where the magic is.


2. Tap to Focus on the Sky, Not the Ground

If you let your phone decide, it’ll expose for the dark desert and turn the sky into a white hole of sadness.

Tap the BRIGHT AREA of the sky.

Your phone instantly:

darkens the shadows
deepens the color
pulls out the reds/oranges

This is the biggest cheat code.


3. Lower Exposure a Tiny Bit

After you tap the sky, slide the brightness down just one notch.

Result:

richer reds
darker silhouettes
crisp cloud detail
total “National Geographic but with beer” vibes


4. Put Something in the Foreground

Sunset + object = instant masterpiece. Try:

windmill
fencepost
wagon
cactus
road sign
the Pathfinder (Stormy!!)

Silhouettes make desert sunsets look cinematic.


5. Wait 10 Minutes After the Sun Actually Sets

The AFTERGLOW is the real show. That’s when New Mexico pulls out the neon pinks and purples.

If you walk away too early, you’ll miss the best part.


6. Hold the Phone Steady

As the sky gets darker, your camera needs stability.

Best method?
Plant your elbows on the Pathfinder roof.
Or on Terri. She doesn’t mind.


7. Take 10–15 Shots — One Will Be Gold

Sunsets change every 15 seconds. Take a few. Pick the best later.

No shame in it. The pros do the same.

Result:
You come home with photos that look EXACTLY like what you saw… and nobody will believe you didn’t use filters.
